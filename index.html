Solving failing modes in Multi-Modal Deep Learning

Typically, a multi-modal neural network exploits information from different channels and in different terms (e.g. images, text, sounds, sensor measures) in the hope that the information carried by each mode is complementary, in order to improve the predictions of the neural network. Nevertheless, in realistic situations, varying levels of perturbations can occur on the data of the modes, which may decrease the quality of the inference process. An additional difficulty is that these perturbations vary between the modes and on a sample-per-sample basis. The proposed solution in this thesis is to enable the neural network focus on the best combination of modes, i.e. the modes containing the least amount of perturbations while having the highest informativeness for the predictive task at hand. Promising results were obtained, as an example, the plot shows the performance evolving when increasing the amount of perturbations on one of the input modes. It can be seen that the performance of the neural network enhanced with our method (blue line) remains stable, whereas the performance using standard data augmentation (most common solution against perturbations, red line) decreases drastically. More information can be found in the report (link below).

Mapping unknown environments using robots

The objective of this project was to build two robots from scratch, exploring an unknown environment as quickly as possible. They are coupled with a program running on my computer during the exploration phase, receiving information messages coming from the robots. Those messages were then used by the program to construct a map of the environment in real-time. A demonstration of the result can be seen below.

I must admit that I underestimated the difficulty of making a robot perform robustly. Despite some moments of frustion caused by hundreds of small problems encountered during the project, it was overall a lot of fun and a great learning experience.
